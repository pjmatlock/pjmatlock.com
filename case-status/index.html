Case Status

United States v. Constantinescu et al., S.D. Tex. (4:22-cr-00612)
Current Status

The indictment was dismissed on March 20, 2024, reversed on appeal on October 2, 2025, and the case is currently pending again in the Southern District of Texas. I am presumed innocent. No evidence has been tested in court.

Timeline

March 20, 2024
Indictment Dismissed
The district court reviewed the government's superseding indictment and dismissed it before trial. The judge found the charges legally insufficient.
October 2, 2025
Fifth Circuit Reversal
The government appealed. The Fifth Circuit reversed the dismissal and remanded on procedural grounds. This was a pleading-stage decision — not a finding of guilt, not a ruling on evidence, and not a determination that any statement was actually false or material.
April 2026
Case Pending — New Judge Assigned
The case is back in the district court for further proceedings. Judge Andrew S. Hanen has recused, and the case is now assigned to Judge Alfred H. Bennett. I maintain my innocence and will continue to fight.

What the Fifth Circuit Did (and Did Not) Decide

The Fifth Circuit's decision was narrow: it addressed only whether the superseding indictment was legally sufficient to move forward at the pleading stage. At this stage, the court accepts the government's allegations as true and asks only whether they state an offense. That is the lowest bar in federal criminal law.

What the Court Did Not Decide

Whether any statement was actually false. Whether any statement was material. Whether any investor was actually defrauded. Whether there was criminal intent. Whether the government can prove any element beyond a reasonable doubt.

All of those questions remain open and will be the government's burden at trial.

Why the Government's Theory Has Problems

I'm not going to lay out my full legal strategy here — that's for court. But I'll say this much: the theory the government is running has serious structural weaknesses that anyone who understands securities law should recognize.

Problem 01

Influence Is Not Deception

The government's theory treats having followers and sharing opinions as evidence of a fraudulent "scheme." But having an audience is not fraud. Posting a bullish view on a stock is not fraud. Influence is not the same as deception, and the law requires actual deception.

Problem 02

Opinions Are Not False Statements

Securities fraud requires a materially false statement of fact. Opinions, predictions, price targets, and "I like this stock" are not factual claims. The government has to identify something objectively false — not just a view someone disagrees with in hindsight.

Problem 03

Materiality Has a Legal Definition

In securities law, "materiality" uses the Supreme Court's "total mix" standard: information is material only if it significantly alters the total mix of information available about the issuer and the security's value. A third party's trading intentions or personal views are not issuer-level information.

Problem 04

Kousisis Was a Different Case

The Fifth Circuit leaned on Kousisis v. United States, a case about lies used to extract contract payments. Applying that framework to public market commentary is a stretch. Open markets with millions of participants are not private contracts where one party controls the terms.

The Bottom Line

A fraud conviction requires proof of a materially false statement, made with criminal intent, that was material to an investment decision under the total mix standard. The government still has to prove all of that — beyond a reasonable doubt — and I believe it cannot.

Why This Matters Beyond My Case

Markets are loud by design. People share bullish and bearish views, post charts, debate catalysts, and change their minds in real time. That is not fraud — that is how markets work.

If prosecutors can treat public trading commentary as a criminal "scheme" without clear, objective falsity and clear materiality, the result is a chilling effect on speech and a blurry line between fraud and disagreement. Every retail trader who posts a take on a stock should care about where this line gets drawn.

This theory doesn't just threaten me. It threatens anyone with a following who talks about markets publicly. If "you had influence and the stock went down after you sold" is enough for a federal indictment, then the rules of engagement for market commentary just changed for everyone.
